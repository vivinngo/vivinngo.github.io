Today, I will introduce the “Fail fast and learn fast” spirit, which can save you from the perfectionist trap and help you move faster in your career.
In my performance review, my line manager said I did well, but I am prone to getting stuck in a loop of improving one specific task, which delays delivery. It is not necessary bad, but as I have many things to do, I need to balance and know the “good-to-go” state to ship things instead of trying to reach the “perfect”. You know, and I also know that “perfect” doesn’t exist, and being down to the perfect loop is just like being in a rabbit hole. That was the first time I heard about the mindset “Fail fast and learn fast”. I was lucky enough to have someone that explains to me the mindset, and for me, and I want to spread this wonderful mindset to you.

The basic idea is that you experiment and test something in the market and go live quickly, then collect data to improve and launch again. It will be a loop, and the developing phase should be quick and simple enough for Minimum Viable Product (MVP) to test the reaction of the market. Of course, it depends on the product. If they are medical products, the research and development phases should be longer and more cautious. The key here is that you need to do it quickly, and also estimate all the possible risks that can happen. Once you can estimate the risks, it gives you peace of mind and more confidence in launching your product.

## Perfect but late is often worse than imperfect but on time
![Alt text](/img/post3_false_fast_learn_fast/example1.jpeg)
In the very first stage of SpaceX, Elon Musk launched rockets with impressive speeds. He launched and learned quickly, with the speed that any space company at that time was shocked. People said, this strategy is usually used in software development, where it is less costly and has zero harm to humans. Yes, the first Falcon 1 was launched and failed. He lost money, but no one was harmed, and he learned a big lesson, which was the foundation of his later success.

“Move fast and break things” is also a motto of Meta, designed by Mark Zuckerberg to prioritize speed, innovation, and rapid iteration over caution. This mindset is adopted mostly in software development, when the cost of failing is cheaper and usually harmless. As you can see, Chat GPT doesn’t wait until model 5.5 to launch, but it launched its first model and improved gradually. What if it waits until the more perfect model finishes? Well, obviously, it would lose its leading position to other companies. But another reason is that it would just be trained in the lab, without real feedback from humans, and its perfect model would not be realistic.

The true cost of waiting is a triple threat: lost market share, wasted effort on trivial features, and resources sunk into a product users simply don’t want.

## Barrier is even bigger with your personal projects
![Alt text](/img/post3_false_fast_learn_fast/example2.jpeg)
At the office, you have managers to push you. At home? You are the boss, the employee, and the critic. When you have an idea to build a YouTube channel or write blogs like me, you feel very excited and enthusiastic at the beginning. But when you start working on it, it’s easy to feel you are not enough and procrastinate launching.

I have that feeling too. I usually wonder if I am good enough to share helpful things with people. In my very first plan for my blog, I planned for some topics that were a lot higher than my skills because I thought people would be interested, and also, I underrated my current knowledge, thinking that I had nothing to share. Some topics took me weeks to understand, let alone write about them. Of course, it helps me learn things, but at the beginning, it held me back from my original goal, writing blogs, because of the long process and a lot of effort.

Here is how I broke the cycle. I’ve started prototyping my thoughts from my experience or new knowledge I just learned. I just open the Medium New Story page and dump my “silly” title and “raw” idea immediately. When there are words on the page, the monster loses its power. Once I polish it, I publish and quickly move on to other topics. I published fast and tested people’s reactions. I will gradually know more about what topics they are interested in by analyzing their interactions on Medium and LinkedIn. On top of that, I can improve my writing skills quickly. I stop wasting weeks on one topic that only I care about.

What is one project you’ve been ‘perfecting’ for too long? Try shipping a draft of it today.
