Everyone knows there are plenty of "under-the-table" jobs, and that startup interview processes are often more straightforward than those in big companies. But wait… do you actually know how to find startups and the best way to approach them to successfully get an offer? This very first step stops many people from landing opportunities, so let me share my step-by-step approach.

A few months ago, I applied to many jobs on LinkedIn and felt discouraged because I didn't receive any responses, let alone an offer. Somehow, I realized this approach was quite passive. Even though I was actively applying, I was doing it at the same time as everyone else on LinkedIn. I believed I could do more, so I started thinking about a more proactive approach. That's how I discovered this method and made it work for me.

In addition to applying to big and medium-sized firms on LinkedIn, I expanded my search to include early-stage startups that don't appear on job boards. I believe this is a great strategy because I can focus on where I could add value and stand out.

## Step 1: Find the right startups

You can find startups in your field through startup competitions. Try searching with keywords like "startup competitions for + your field" or "top promising startups in + your field." Then, look for the names of top winners or finalists from those competitions. Some popular sources include Future of Capitalism Competition, Startups Magazine, Startup World Cup, Oxford Founders Society, Oxford Seed Fund, FounderMatcha, etc.

Another great way to proactively approach startups is by attending meetups and events in your city. You can easily find these gatherings on Meetup.com or LinkedIn. Go out, talk to people, and listen to what they do; you might be surprised by how many opportunities come from simple conversations. My classmate got an internship thanks to a conversation with a CEO at an event.

Furthermore, you can also find startups through university and alumni pages. These pages often highlight startups founded by their students or alumni, sometimes as job posts, other times as proud success stories. Follow these pages and take note of startups working in your area of interest.

One important tip: don't limit yourself to your own university. Try following other universities as well. For example, I found my current company through a post on an Oxford University page, even though I don't study at Oxford.

## Step 2: Match their needs with your strengths

After identifying some promising startups in your field, the next step is to understand their work and needs, and reflect on how your abilities align. For example, you can visit the "People" section on their LinkedIn company page to see what roles they currently have. If you find positions similar to what you're looking for, that's a great sign. You can also explore their website to learn more about what they do, or even message them directly to express your interest and suggest a short call to learn more about their work. People love talking about what they do, especially those in startups, trust me.

Now, let's reflect on your abilities. Based on what the startup does, what can you offer to help them? For instance, if they're working on AI/ML products, one of the biggest and most time-consuming tasks is data processing. As a graduate or intern, you could offer something that supports them. Be specific about what you can actually do, and present it in an engaging, confident way.

## Step 3: Send a cold email / message to offer your value

When reaching out to a startup, you can start by introducing yourself and mentioning what you're studying or your current experience, and briefly highlighting your area of expertise or passion. Then, explain why you're interested in their company. It could be their mission, product, or the problem they're trying to solve. Showing that you've done your homework helps you stand out from a generic applicant.

Next, offer specific ways you can contribute as an intern or part-time team member. Instead of saying "I want to gain experience," focus on what you can bring to them. For example, "I can support your team in data analysis, automate manual processes, or help with customer research." Be clear and confident about how your skills align with their work.

## Step 4: Keep in touch even without an offer

Try to connect with people in those companies, especially founders, co-founders, and HR professionals. Stay engaged by occasionally commenting on their posts or congratulating them on the company's achievements. Even if you don't receive an offer, it's still valuable to maintain those connections within your field. You never know when a future opportunity might arise.
